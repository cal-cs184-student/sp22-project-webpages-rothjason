Task 1

  • To rasterize a triangle, my initial idea was to iterate through each each pixel, and perform a “point-in-triangle” test. To do this, I would have to form a vector between each set of points given for the triangle, and perform the half-plane test between the point and the created vector. After finding the normal for each vector, I could simply dot-product the normal vector and the vector formed by <x-xi, y-yi>, where (x, y) is the pixel’s position in question, and (xi, yi) is vertex in the triangle. To ensure that the point-in-triangle test would work regardless of the winding of the triangle’s vertices, I performed the half-plane test twice, one checking if the point was above all the lines, and once again checking if the point was not above any of the plains. Since the ladder condition implies the first with opposite winding and they are mutually exclusive, it would always be able to detect if the point was in the triangle.
  • To ensure that my algorithm was fast and no worse than the bounding box method described in class, I created my own bounding box for the triangle rather than checking every pixel from 0 to max width/height. To create the bounds, I simply found the mix/max values between the three triangle vertices for both X and Y coordinates. This allowed me to start the double for loop at xmin, ymin, and terminate at xmax, ymax, thus covering the exact square with vertices touching the borders of the bound.

Task 2

  • For task 2, I primary worked with the sample_buffer vector and scaled it up by a factor of the sample_rate. I did this so I could sample more points and average out the points for smoother edges. Supersampling is useful for hiding jaggies and other aliasing like floating points on sharp corners. In the pipeline, I modified the rasterize_triangle and resolve_to_framebuffer methods. For the former, I used the scaled up sample_buffer to sample n by n points per sample, in order to average out the colors in the sample. Once I finished this supersampling, I used modified resolve_to_framebuffer to average out these supersampled points. To implement the super sampling an resolving, I added two more inner for loops (so total 4 for loops), in order to process an extra n by n points per sample, and average these out during the resolve.

As the sampling increases, the the corner of this triangle slowly is blended more into the white background. This prevents the floating-pixels (aliasing) from occurring. This happens because rather than being forced to chose 1 color or the other (red vs. white in this case), the sampling rate allow for multiple samples to be taken and then averaged for each pixel.

Task 5

  • Pixel sampling is the process of sampling a color from a given texture. To do this, we need to translate our screen pixels to locations on the texture, by use of barymetric coordinates. Once we have coordinates for the texture (u,v), we need to sample the color of the texture. We have two ways to do this: nearest and bilinear. For nearest, we simply can round u,v to the nearest integer, giving us the closest pixel color from the texture map. With bilinear, we first find the closest 4 pixels, and sample their colors. We then perform two sets of linear interpolation in order to average out the colors between the pixels, giving us an “average” color of where this pixel, even if it isn’t a perfect integer.

Bilinear sampling greatly helps reduce jaggies without too much blur, whereas supersampling seems to induce a larger amount of blur. This is especially noticeable in the thinner lines, such as the lines of latitude.

Task 6

  • In level sampling, we determine which mip-map is appropriate the sample from for our given texture. This is combined with the pixel sampling to either double the pixel sampling and average it out (trilinear), or simply find the best approximated mip-map and perform the pixel-sampling of choice. I implemented this with the user of the sample helper function, which handled the control flow from the sample parameters. To calculate what level(s) should be used for the sampling, I implemented the helper function get_level, which would calculate the D (float) as described in the lecture. With this D, for trilinear I would perform another lerp between the colors obtain from the floor and ceiling of D and sampled to their respective pixel-sampling type (described by the sample-params arg). If it was neared, instead of calculating the lower and upper D integers and lerp-ing them together, I would simply round D and perform the sample and level round(D).
  • While trilinear (P_LINEAR, L_LINEAR) theoretically gets one of the best samples, it takes up far more computer power because it needs to sample more times than the other combinations, and average them out. It also takes up more memory, since it needs to hold the values between calculations.
